A black hole is a region in space where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from it. It is formed when a massive star collapses under its own gravity after exhausting its nuclear fuel. Because light cannot escape, black holes appear completely black and can only be detected through their effects on nearby objects. Key Features of a Black Hole 1. Singularity – The core of a black hole where gravity is infinitely strong and space-time curves infinitely. 2. Event Horizon – The boundary around a black hole beyond which nothing can return; also called the “point of no return.” 3. Accretion Disk – A rotating disk of gas and dust that forms around a black hole as matter falls in, heating up and emitting X-rays. 4. Hawking Radiation – A theoretical process by which black holes can slowly lose mass and energy due to quantum effects. Types of Black Holes 1. Stellar Black Holes – Formed from the collapse of massive stars (3–20 times the Sun’s mass). 2. Supermassive Black Holes – Found at the center of galaxies, millions to billions of times the Sun’s mass (e.g., Sagittarius A* in the Milky Way). 3. Intermediate Black Holes – Mid-sized black holes, possibly formed by merging stellar black holes. 4. Primordial Black Holes – Hypothetical black holes formed in the early universe. Black holes play a crucial role in understanding general relativity, quantum mechanics, and the evolution of galaxies.
